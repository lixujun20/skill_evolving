# Skill Evolution Story: Reusability, EM-style Maintenance, and Textual Meta-Learning

Date: 2026-05-21

## 1. Core Thesis

The central object of test-time skill evolution should not be an isolated skill
that solves a past task. The central object is a **maintainable skill library**
whose value is measured by future reuse.

A skill is useful only insofar as it compresses repeated execution structure
across tasks. In this view, skill evolution is not one-shot extraction from a
trace. It is an online software-engineering loop that repeatedly:

1. observes how the current skill library changes execution,
2. infers which latent skill-use decisions were helpful or harmful,
3. updates the repository to increase future expected utility, and
4. updates the role prompts that generate and maintain skills.

This gives a unified story:

- **Repository evolution** is an EM-style latent-variable optimization problem.
- **Role improvement** is textual meta-learning over the approximate EM
  operators.
- **Reusability** is the criterion that connects both layers.

## 2. Why This Is Not Classical EM

Classical EM assumes fixed observed data `X`, latent variables `Z`, and model
parameters `\theta`. It maximizes the marginal likelihood:

$$
\log p_\theta(X)
=
\log \int p_\theta(X,Z)\,dZ.
$$

The E-step computes a posterior under the current parameter:

$$
q^{(t)}(Z)
=
p_{\theta^{(t)}}(Z \mid X).
$$

The M-step maximizes the expected complete-data log likelihood:

$$
\theta^{(t+1)}
=
\arg\max_\theta
\mathbb{E}_{Z \sim q^{(t)}}[
\log p_\theta(X,Z)
].
$$

Our setting is different. The task/query distribution is fixed, but execution
traces are not fixed observations. A trace is generated by the executor under
the current skill library. Therefore the trace distribution itself depends on
the current repository:

$$
x,z \sim p_\theta(x,z \mid q).
$$

Here:

- `q` is a task/query.
- `\theta` is the skill repository.
- `x` is the execution trace.
- `z` is the latent skill-use structure: retrieved skills, injected skills,
  directly called skills, copied/adapted skills, ignored-but-relevant skills,
  harmful skills, and credit assignments.

Therefore we are not maximizing likelihood of a fixed `X`. We are optimizing
the expected utility of traces induced by the current skill library:

$$
J(\theta)
=
\mathbb{E}_{q \sim \mathcal{D}}
\mathbb{E}_{x,z \sim p_\theta(x,z \mid q)}
\left[
U(q,x,z;\theta)
\right]
-
\lambda \Omega(\theta).
$$

`U` measures correctness, efficiency, robustness, future reusability, and
negative side effects. `\Omega` regularizes repository size, redundancy, prompt
cost, dependency complexity, and retrieval noise.

This is better described as **online generalized EM-style optimization**, not
standard maximum-likelihood EM.

## 3. Generalized EM: What Is Mathematically Legitimate?

Generalized EM (GEM) is a standard term in the EM literature. In classical EM,
the M-step must exactly maximize the EM auxiliary objective. In GEM, the M-step
only needs to improve it, or approximately improve it:

$$
Q(\theta \mid \theta^{(t)})
=
\mathbb{E}_{Z \sim p_{\theta^{(t)}}(Z \mid X)}
\left[
\log p_\theta(X,Z)
\right].
$$

Classical EM requires:

$$
\theta^{(t+1)}
=
\arg\max_\theta Q(\theta \mid \theta^{(t)}).
$$

Generalized EM only requires:

$$
Q(\theta^{(t+1)} \mid \theta^{(t)})
\ge
Q(\theta^{(t)} \mid \theta^{(t)}).
$$

So "generalized EM" has a real mathematical meaning, but it still usually
refers to latent-variable likelihood objectives.

For our method, the most defensible wording is:

> The algorithm can be interpreted as an online generalized EM-style procedure
> for maximizing expected utility under latent skill-use assignments.

This wording is intentionally careful:

- **EM-style**: because we alternate between inferring latent skill-use
  variables and updating the skill library.
- **Generalized**: because the M-step is an approximate maintenance operator,
  not an exact optimizer.
- **Expected utility**: because the target is not fixed-data likelihood.
- **Online**: because traces are endogenous samples generated by the current
  repository.

EM can be generalized beyond maximum likelihood in a broad sense through
variational inference, expectation-maximization over surrogate objectives, and
coordinate-ascent views. But if the paper simply says "this is EM", reviewers
may object. The safer claim is that our algorithm is **motivated by the EM
decomposition** and instantiates a **GEM-like coordinate optimization loop**.

## 4. EM-style Formalization for Skill Libraries

Let the current repository be `\theta^{(t)}`. For a task `q_i`, the executor
rolls out a trace:

$$
x_i^{(t)} \sim p_{\theta^{(t)}}(x \mid q_i).
$$

The latent variable `z_i` describes how the skill library should have been
used in that trace. It includes:

$$
z_i =
(
r_i,
a_i,
c_i,
h_i,
m_i
),
$$

where:

- `r_i`: retrieved skills,
- `a_i`: injected or exposed skills,
- `c_i`: directly called, copied, or adapted skills,
- `h_i`: helpful/harmful/neutral credit labels,
- `m_i`: maintenance decisions such as refine, merge, promote, disable, or
  add test.

The ideal E-step would compute:

$$
p_{\theta^{(t)}}(z_i \mid q_i, x_i^{(t)}, Y_i=1),
$$

where `Y_i=1` denotes high-quality execution. Since this posterior is
intractable, the system approximates it with verifier signals, trace projection,
retrieval logs, credit assignment, and bundle-test evidence:

$$
\hat{q}^{(t)}(z_i)
\approx
p_{\theta^{(t)}}(z_i \mid q_i, x_i^{(t)}, \hat{U}_i).
$$

The E-step in our algorithm is therefore:

$$
\text{E-step: }
(x_i^{(t)}, \hat{q}^{(t)}(z_i), \hat{U}_i)
\leftarrow
\text{ExecuteAndCredit}(q_i,\theta^{(t)}).
$$

The M-step updates the repository:

$$
\theta^{(t+1)}
\leftarrow
\text{Maintain}
\left(
\theta^{(t)},
\{q_i,x_i^{(t)},\hat{q}^{(t)}(z_i),\hat{U}_i\}_{i \in W_t}
\right).
$$

The maintenance operator is an approximate maximizer of future expected utility:

$$
\theta^{(t+1)}
\approx
\arg\max_\theta
\sum_{i \in W_t}
\mathbb{E}_{z_i \sim \hat{q}^{(t)}}
\left[
\widehat{U}(q_i,x_i^{(t)},z_i;\theta)
\right]
-
\lambda \Omega(\theta).
$$

In implementation, this approximate M-step consists of software-maintenance
actions:

- extract new reusable skills from successful or diagnostic traces,
- refine relevant-but-unused skills so that future executors can adopt them,
- add bundle tests from positive, negative, and integration evidence,
- promote pending skills only with posterior helpful evidence,
- merge or refactor overlapping skills,
- disable skills with harmful or irrelevant retrieval evidence.

## 5. Where TRL / Meta-RL Fits

The EM-style view above treats the repository `\theta` as the optimized
object. But the operators that perform the E-step and M-step are themselves
LLM-powered roles:

- executor,
- retriever / injector,
- credit assigner,
- extractor,
- refiner,
- bundle tester,
- macro refactorer.

Let `\phi` denote the textual policies controlling these roles. In practice,
`\phi` is a set of prompt rules, rubrics, examples, and role-specific
instructions.

Then the full system has two coupled parameters:

$$
(\theta,\phi).
$$

- `\theta`: the current skill library.
- `\phi`: the textual policy that determines how the system executes the
  approximate E-step and M-step.

The outer objective is:

$$
J(\theta,\phi)
=
\mathbb{E}_{q \sim \mathcal{D}}
\mathbb{E}_{x,z \sim p_{\theta,\phi}(x,z \mid q)}
\left[
U(q,x,z;\theta)
\right]
-
\lambda \Omega(\theta)
-
\gamma R(\phi).
$$

`R(\phi)` regularizes role-policy complexity, prompt length, overfitting, and
unstable rules.

The role-improvement loop is a textual analogue of policy optimization:

$$
\phi^{(t+1)}
\leftarrow
\text{TextualPolicyUpdate}
\left(
\phi^{(t)},
\mathcal{F}^{(t)}
\right),
$$

where `\mathcal{F}^{(t)}` is runtime feedback about generated skills:

- which extracted skills were never used,
- which skills were retrieved but ignored,
- which skills were harmful,
- which skills passed tests but failed future use,
- which skill candidates were promoted,
- which extractor/refiner decisions produced reusable artifacts.

This is similar to meta-RL:

- policy: role prompt `\phi`,
- state: repository state, generated skills, trace evidence, and maintenance
  history,
- action: textual generation/refinement decisions made by roles,
- reward: future skill utility, posterior reuse, correctness, and token saving,
- policy update: semantic rule-of-thumb changes appended to role prompts.

It is not gradient descent through model weights. It is a **semantic policy
gradient** in text space:

$$
\nabla_\phi J
\quad \text{is approximated by} \quad
\Delta \phi =
\text{LLM-SummarizeFailureAndSuccessRules}(\mathcal{F}^{(t)}).
$$

The update does not produce numeric gradients. It produces executable textual
rules such as:

- "Do not extract task-specific answer scripts as reusable spreadsheet skills."
- "When a callable skill is retrieved but unused despite semantic relevance,
  refine the signature and applicability section."
- "Prefer skills whose body describes a reusable invariant rather than a
  workbook-specific range."

## 6. Unified Algorithmic Story

The cleanest story is a two-level online optimization process.

### Inner Loop: EM-style Repository Maintenance

For fixed role policies `\phi`, the system alternates between:

1. **E-step / inference**

   Roll out the executor under the current repository and infer latent skill-use
   assignments:

   $$
   (x,z,\hat{U})
   \leftarrow
   \text{ExecuteAndCredit}(q,\theta,\phi).
   $$

2. **M-step / repository update**

   Update the skill repository through extraction, refinement, testing,
   promotion, merging, and filtering:

   $$
   \theta
   \leftarrow
   \text{Maintain}(\theta,x,z,\hat{U},\phi).
   $$

This layer answers:

> Given the current role behavior, what skill library best compresses observed
> and expected future reuse?

### Outer Loop: Textual Role Learning

The outer loop observes failures and successes of the inner-loop maintenance
operators and updates `\phi`:

$$
\phi
\leftarrow
\text{UpdateRolePolicies}(\phi,\text{skill-performance feedback}).
$$

This layer answers:

> How should the extractor, credit assigner, and refiner change their behavior
> so that future EM-style updates produce more reusable skills?

Thus the whole method is not "EM plus unrelated TRL". It is:

> Online generalized EM over the skill library, where the approximate E/M
> operators are themselves improved by textual meta-learning from runtime
> evidence.

## 7. Suggested Paper Wording

Recommended concise formulation:

> We formulate test-time skill evolution as online utility maximization over a
> versioned skill repository. The repository controls the distribution of future
> execution traces, while each trace contains latent skill-use assignments: which
> skills should be retrieved, exposed, called, adapted, repaired, or filtered.
> This induces an EM-style view. The E-step performs on-policy execution under
> the current repository and infers latent credit variables from verifier,
> retrieval, and usage evidence. The M-step performs a generalized repository
> update: it extracts new reusable skills, repairs relevant-but-unused skills,
> promotes posterior-helpful candidates, merges overlapping artifacts, and
> disables harmful ones. Since these E/M operators are implemented by LLM roles,
> we further add a textual meta-learning loop that updates role prompts from
> downstream skill utility. The result is a closed-loop software-engineering
> system for optimizing reusable skills rather than a one-shot skill extraction
> pipeline.

## 8. Important Caveats

Do not claim the algorithm is exactly classical EM unless the objective is
rewritten as a fixed-data latent-variable likelihood. The safer framing is:

- "EM-style"
- "generalized EM-inspired"
- "coordinate optimization over latent skill-use assignments"
- "online expected-utility optimization"

Also avoid implying monotonic improvement is guaranteed. Because traces are
sampled on-policy and the M-step is implemented by LLM maintenance operators,
the method is approximate and stochastic. The rigorous claim should be that the
algorithm is designed to improve a test-time expected-utility surrogate and is
validated empirically.

## 9. How the Two Technical Points Fit the Story

### Reusability-centered repository maintenance

This is the inner-loop M-step. It supplies the software-engineering discipline:

- prior extraction from traces,
- posterior credit from real use,
- relevant-but-unused refinement,
- short-term micro maintenance,
- long-term macro refactoring,
- bundle tests and regression evidence,
- filtering of harmful or noisy skills.

The key novelty is that skill quality is judged by reuse evidence, not by the
historical task alone.

### Runtime role improvement / TRL

This is the outer-loop policy improvement over `\phi`. It does not replace
repository maintenance. It improves the generators and judges that implement
maintenance.

Its reward is not a single task score. The reward is downstream skill behavior:

- whether generated skills are retrieved,
- whether retrieved skills are used,
- whether used skills improve correctness or cost,
- whether candidates survive testing and promotion,
- whether they remain useful after future tasks.

This makes TRL a meta-maintenance mechanism: it learns how to perform better
future E/M updates.

## 10. One-sentence Story

We optimize a reusable skill library with an online generalized EM-style loop,
and we optimize the LLM roles that approximate this loop with textual
meta-learning from downstream skill utility.

## 11. Surrogate Objective and Correctness Argument

### 11.1 Why We Need a New Proof Argument

The classical EM proof cannot be directly reused because our objective is not
the likelihood of fixed observations. Classical EM optimizes:

$$
\log p_\theta(X)
$$

where `X` is fixed. Our objective is instead expected utility under traces
induced by the current skill library:

$$
J(\theta)
=
\mathbb{E}_{q \sim \mathcal{D}}
\mathbb{E}_{x,z \sim p_\theta(x,z \mid q)}
\left[
U(q,x,z;\theta)
\right]
-
\lambda \Omega(\theta).
$$

Here, both the trace `x` and latent skill-use assignment `z` depend on the
current repository. Therefore the right proof template is not the likelihood
lower-bound proof of EM, but a **surrogate-improvement proof** in the spirit of
minorization-maximization or generalized coordinate ascent.

The main claim should be:

> If the maintenance step improves a credit-weighted utility surrogate, and the
> surrogate is a sufficiently accurate local approximation to future expected
> utility, then the repository update improves true expected utility up to
> attribution, optimization, and sampling errors.

### 11.2 What Is the Surrogate?

At round `t`, the current repository is `\theta_t`. For tasks in the current
online window `W_t`, the executor samples traces:

$$
x_i^{(t)} \sim p_{\theta_t}(x \mid q_i).
$$

The credit assigner estimates a posterior-like distribution over latent
skill-use explanations:

$$
\hat{q}_t(z_i)
\approx
p_{\theta_t}(z_i \mid q_i, x_i^{(t)}, \hat{U}_i).
$$

The **surrogate** is the empirical, credit-weighted estimate of the utility that
a candidate repository `\theta` would deliver on the trace evidence collected
under `\theta_t`:

$$
\widehat{Q}_t(\theta)
=
\frac{1}{|W_t|}
\sum_{i \in W_t}
\mathbb{E}_{z_i \sim \hat{q}_t}
\left[
\widehat{U}(q_i, x_i^{(t)}, z_i; \theta)
\right]
-
\lambda \Omega(\theta).
$$

This is the key object. It is not a likelihood lower bound. It is a local
expected-utility surrogate built from:

1. **On-policy trace samples** `x_i^{(t)}`: what the current repository caused
   the executor to do.
2. **Latent skill-use estimates** `\hat{q}_t(z_i)`: credit assignment over
   helpful, harmful, unused-but-relevant, irrelevant, and missing skills.
3. **Counterfactual repository utility** `\widehat{U}(\cdot;\theta)`: how good
   a proposed repository would be if it preserved, repaired, generalized, or
   removed the implicated skills.
4. **Repository regularization** `\Omega(\theta)`: cost for bloat, redundancy,
   prompt length, retrieval pollution, dependency complexity, and stale skills.

A concrete decomposition is:

$$
\widehat{U}(q_i,x_i,z_i;\theta)
=
\alpha_{\mathrm{task}} \widehat{R}_{\mathrm{task}}
+
\alpha_{\mathrm{reuse}} \widehat{R}_{\mathrm{reuse}}
+
\alpha_{\mathrm{cost}} \widehat{R}_{\mathrm{cost}}
-
\alpha_{\mathrm{harm}} \widehat{C}_{\mathrm{harm}}.
$$

Where:

- `\widehat{R}_{\mathrm{task}}`: verifier score or success signal on the
  current task/trace.
- `\widehat{R}_{\mathrm{reuse}}`: posterior evidence that a skill supports
  future related tasks, such as repeated helpful credit, positive bundle cases,
  or relevant-but-unused refinement opportunities.
- `\widehat{R}_{\mathrm{cost}}`: token savings, fewer execution steps, fewer
  failed tool calls, or simpler executor behavior.
- `\widehat{C}_{\mathrm{harm}}`: harmful credit, irrelevant retrieval,
  workflow pollution, execution errors, or misleading scope.

The M-step approximately solves:

$$
\theta_{t+1}
\approx
\arg\max_\theta \widehat{Q}_t(\theta).
$$

In practice this is implemented by repository maintenance:

$$
\theta_{t+1}
=
\text{Maintain}(\theta_t, W_t).
$$

The minimum correctness condition is surrogate improvement:

$$
\widehat{Q}_t(\theta_{t+1})
\ge
\widehat{Q}_t(\theta_t)
-
\epsilon_{\mathrm{opt}}.
$$

`\epsilon_{\mathrm{opt}}` captures imperfect LLM maintenance, failed repairs,
or conservative decisions that keep a skill unchanged.

### 11.3 Relation to Classical EM

Typora often fails to render LaTeX inside Markdown tables, so the comparison is
written as a list instead of a table.

**Optimized object**

- Classical EM: model parameter.
- Our method: skill repository, and optionally role policy.

**Data**

- Classical EM: fixed observations.
- Our method: online tasks are fixed inputs; traces are generated by the current
  repository.

**Latent variable**

- Classical EM: missing data.
- Our method: skill-use assignment, including retrieved, exposed, called,
  copied, ignored, helpful, harmful, and missing skills.

**Objective**

- Classical EM: marginal likelihood.

$$
\log p_\theta(X)
$$

- Our method: expected utility under the induced trace distribution.

$$
J(\theta)
$$

**E-step**

- Classical EM: compute the posterior over hidden variables.

$$
p_{\theta_t}(Z \mid X)
$$

- Our method: roll out the executor under the current repository, then estimate
  latent skill-use credit.

$$
\hat{q}_t(z \mid q,x,\hat{U})
$$

**E-step output**

- Classical EM: posterior over hidden variables.
- Our method: trace, verifier result, retrieval/use logs, credit labels, and
  bundle suggestions.

**Surrogate**

- Classical EM:

$$
Q(\theta \mid \theta_t)
=
\mathbb{E}[\log p_\theta(X,Z)]
$$

- Our method:

$$
\widehat{Q}_t(\theta)
=
\frac{1}{\lvert W_t \rvert}
\sum_i
\mathbb{E}_{z_i\sim\hat q_t}
[
\widehat U(q_i,x_i,z_i;\theta)
]
-
\lambda\Omega(\theta)
$$

**M-step**

- Classical EM: maximize the likelihood surrogate.
- Our method: maintain the repository to improve the utility surrogate.

**Exact guarantee**

- Classical EM: monotonic likelihood increase under exact E/M steps.
- Our method: surrogate improvement; true utility improves up to estimation,
  optimization, and sampling error.

**Failure mode**

- Classical EM: bad local optimum or approximate E/M error.
- Our method: credit error, non-representative window, LLM maintenance error,
  overbroad skills, or retrieval pollution.

**Why it is still EM-like**

- Classical EM: alternates posterior inference and parameter update.
- Our method: alternates latent skill-use inference and repository update.

### 11.4 Correctness Statement

Let the true expected utility objective be `J(\theta)`. Let
`\widehat{Q}_t(\theta)` be the window-level surrogate defined above. Suppose
that, for candidate repositories considered by the maintenance operator, the
surrogate approximates the local true objective within error
`\epsilon_{\mathrm{est}}+\epsilon_{\mathrm{gen}}`:

$$
\left|
J(\theta)
-
\widehat{Q}_t(\theta)
\right|
\le
\epsilon_{\mathrm{est}}
+
\epsilon_{\mathrm{gen}}.
$$

Here:

- `\epsilon_{\mathrm{est}}` is credit/utility estimation error.
- `\epsilon_{\mathrm{gen}}` is the generalization gap between the current
  online window and the future task distribution.

If the maintenance step improves the surrogate by at least `\Delta_t` up to
optimization error:

$$
\widehat{Q}_t(\theta_{t+1})
-
\widehat{Q}_t(\theta_t)
\ge
\Delta_t
-
\epsilon_{\mathrm{opt}},
$$

then the true expected utility satisfies:

$$
J(\theta_{t+1})
-
J(\theta_t)
\ge
\Delta_t
-
\epsilon_{\mathrm{opt}}
-
2\epsilon_{\mathrm{est}}
-
2\epsilon_{\mathrm{gen}}.
$$

This is the appropriate analogue of the EM monotonicity argument. It does not
claim exact monotonic improvement. It says that when credit assignment is
accurate, the online window is representative, and maintenance improves the
surrogate enough, the repository update improves true expected utility.

### 11.5 Why the Surrogate Matches Our System Design

The surrogate explains why each engineering component is necessary:

- **Credit assignment** estimates `\hat{q}_t(z)`, the latent skill-use
  explanation.
- **Retrieved-but-unused refinement** handles the latent case where a skill is
  semantically relevant but not adoptable by the executor.
- **Bundle tests** make `\widehat{U}` less dependent on verbal credit alone by
  converting trace evidence into executable contracts.
- **Promotion** requires posterior helpful evidence, preventing source-task-only
  overfitting.
- **Filtering** penalizes skills that create negative utility through retrieval
  pollution or harmful execution.
- **Macro refactoring** optimizes the regularized repository objective by
  reducing redundancy and consolidating reusable structure.
- **TRL / textual role update** reduces future `\epsilon_{\mathrm{est}}` and
  `\epsilon_{\mathrm{opt}}` by making the credit assigner, extractor, and
  refiner better approximate the desired E/M operators.

### 11.6 Suggested Short Paper Paragraph

> Since our objective is expected utility rather than likelihood, we do not
> claim classical EM monotonicity. Instead, our algorithm follows a stochastic
> generalized EM or minorization-maximization template. At each window, the
> current repository induces execution traces; the credit assigner estimates a
> posterior-like distribution over latent skill-use assignments; and maintenance
> improves a credit-weighted utility surrogate over candidate repositories. If
> this surrogate is an accurate local approximation to future utility and the
> maintenance step improves it, then true expected utility improves up to
> attribution, optimization, and sampling errors. This justifies the closed-loop
> design: repository edits are accepted only when supported by credit evidence,
> executable bundle tests, posterior reuse, or negative evidence for filtering.
